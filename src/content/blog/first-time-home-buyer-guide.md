Buying your first home is exciting and overwhelming in equal measure. Most guides cover individual pieces. This one covers the entire journey — in the right order — so you know exactly what to do and when.

## Step 1: Check your financial readiness (3–6 months before)

Before looking at a single home, assess your financial position honestly.

**Credit score:** Pull your free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com. You want a minimum of 620 for conventional loans, 580 for FHA. Ideally 720+ for the best rates. If below 680, spend 6–12 months improving it before applying.

**Debt-to-income ratio:** Add up all monthly debt payments (car, student loans, credit cards, minimum payments) and divide by gross monthly income. Keep this under 36% before adding a mortgage payment.

**Down payment + reserves:** Beyond your down payment, lenders want to see 2–3 months of mortgage payments in savings after closing. Calculate total cash needed: down payment + closing costs (2–5% of loan) + reserve funds.

**Employment stability:** Most lenders want 2 years of consistent employment in the same field. Job changes within the same industry are usually fine; career changes can complicate approval.

## Step 2: Get pre-approved (not just pre-qualified)

**Pre-qualification** is a rough estimate based on information you provide. **Pre-approval** is a full credit check and document review — the only one that matters to sellers.

Documents you'll need:
- Last 2 years of tax returns (W-2s and 1040s)
- Last 2 months of pay stubs
- Last 2–3 months of bank statements
- Last 2 years of W-2s
- Photo ID
- List of debts and assets

Get pre-approved at **2–3 lenders simultaneously** — multiple mortgage inquiries within a 45-day window count as a single inquiry on your credit report. Rate shopping costs you nothing.

## Step 3: Define your actual budget

Your pre-approval amount is the maximum you qualify for — not what you should spend. Lenders approve you up to their DTI limits, which often feel uncomfortable in practice.

Use our [affordability calculator](/affordability) to find your **comfortable** budget — not your maximum. Factor in:
- PITI payment (not just P&I)
- Maintenance (budget 1–2% of home value annually)
- Utilities (often higher than renting)
- HOA fees if applicable
- Lifestyle costs you don't want to sacrifice

<div class="tip"><strong>The 25% rule:</strong> Many financial experts suggest keeping housing costs under 25% of take-home pay (not gross income). This is more conservative than lender guidelines but leaves significant breathing room.</div>

## Step 4: Find a buyer's agent

A buyer's agent represents your interests — and in most transactions, is paid by the seller's proceeds. Find an agent who:
- Specializes in your target neighborhoods
- Has at least 3–5 years of full-time experience
- Communicates in your preferred style (text, call, email)
- Is not pushing you toward the top of your budget

Interview 2–3 agents before committing. Ask: "How many buyers did you represent last year?" and "What's your list-to-sale price ratio for buyers?"

## Step 5: House hunting — what to prioritize

You won't find a perfect home. Prioritize ruthlessly:

**Things you cannot change:**
- Location and neighborhood
- School district
- Lot size and orientation
- Street noise / traffic
- Neighbor density

**Things you can change:**
- Kitchen and bathrooms (expensive but doable)
- Flooring, paint, fixtures
- Landscaping
- Interior layout (sometimes)

Look past staging and cosmetics. Focus on bones: foundation, roof age, HVAC age, plumbing type, electrical panel capacity.

## Step 6: Making an offer

In competitive markets, your first offer must be strong. Elements of a purchase offer:

- **Purchase price** — based on comparable sales (comps) from your agent
- **Earnest money deposit** — typically 1–3% of purchase price, shows good faith
- **Contingencies** — financing, inspection, appraisal (these protect you but weaken the offer)
- **Closing date** — flexibility often valued by sellers
- **What's included** — appliances, fixtures, personal property

<div class="warning"><strong>Waiving contingencies:</strong> In hot markets, buyers sometimes waive inspection or appraisal contingencies. This is risky. Understand what you're giving up before waiving any protection.</div>

## Step 7: Home inspection

Always get an inspection — even in "as-is" sales. Cost: $300–600. Worth every dollar.

A good inspector checks: foundation, roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical, insulation, windows, and more. Attend the inspection yourself and ask questions.

After inspection, you can:
- Accept the home as-is
- Request repairs before closing
- Ask for a price reduction in lieu of repairs
- Walk away if issues are too significant

Major red flags: foundation cracks, active water intrusion, outdated electrical (knob-and-tube or aluminum wiring), galvanized steel plumbing, and HVAC systems near end of life.

## Step 8: Appraisal and final loan approval

After the offer is accepted, your lender orders an appraisal ($400–700). The appraiser assesses market value based on comparable sales.

**If it appraises at or above purchase price:** Smooth sailing — proceed to closing.

**If it appraises below purchase price (appraisal gap):** You have options: renegotiate the price down, pay the difference in cash, or walk away (if you have an appraisal contingency).

While awaiting appraisal, your lender completes underwriting — the final deep dive into your finances. Do NOT make any major financial changes during this period: no new credit cards, no large purchases, no job changes.

## Step 9: Final walkthrough and closing

**48 hours before closing:** Do a final walkthrough to confirm agreed repairs were made and the home is in the expected condition.

**At closing:**
- Bring government-issued photo ID
- Bring cashier's check or confirm wire transfer
- Expect to sign 40–100 pages of documents
- Review HUD-1/Closing Disclosure carefully — verify numbers match your Loan Estimate

Once signed: you own a home.
