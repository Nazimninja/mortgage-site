Mortgage points (also called discount points) let you prepay interest to get a lower rate. Sounds good — but whether it actually saves money depends entirely on how long you stay in the home.

## What are mortgage points?

One mortgage point = 1% of your loan amount paid at closing. In exchange, your lender reduces your interest rate — typically by 0.25% per point, though this varies.

**Example:** On a $400,000 loan:
- 1 point = $4,000 paid at closing
- Rate reduction: 7.0% → 6.75%
- Monthly savings: $57/month

## The break-even calculation

**Break-even = Cost of points ÷ Monthly savings**

$4,000 ÷ $57 = **70 months (5.8 years)**

If you stay beyond 5.8 years → buying points was profitable.
If you sell or refinance before 5.8 years → you lost money.

## Break-even by point scenario

Based on $400,000 loan, starting rate 7.0%, typical 0.25% reduction per point:

| Points | Upfront Cost | New Rate | Monthly Savings | Break-Even |
|--------|-------------|---------|----------------|-----------|
| 0.5 | $2,000 | 6.875% | $28/mo | 71 months |
| 1.0 | $4,000 | 6.75% | $57/mo | 70 months |
| 2.0 | $8,000 | 6.5% | $113/mo | 71 months |
| 3.0 | $12,000 | 6.25% | $169/mo | 71 months |

Notice: break-even is similar regardless of how many points you buy. The question is simply: will you stay 6+ years?

## When buying points makes sense

**Long-term homeowners:** If you're buying your forever home or plan to stay 10+ years, buying 1–2 points is almost always worth it. The longer you stay, the more you save.

**High-rate environment:** In a 7%+ rate environment, every fraction of a percent saved matters more in absolute dollars. Points are more valuable when rates are high.

**You have the cash:** Only buy points if you have sufficient cash for the down payment AND closing costs AND points AND 3-month reserve. Don't drain reserves to buy points.

**Points are tax-deductible:** Discount points paid on a home purchase (not refinance) are generally fully deductible in the year paid if you itemize. This effectively reduces the break-even period.

## When buying points is a bad idea

**You might refinance:** If there's any chance you'll refinance within 3–5 years (rates drop, life changes), buying points now means paying twice — once for this loan's points and once for the refinance.

**You might move:** Job relocations, family changes, career pivots. If there's meaningful probability of moving within 5 years, skip the points.

**You need the cash:** Buying points is never worth sacrificing your emergency fund or down payment.

**You're getting an ARM:** No sense buying points on a loan you'll refinance before adjustment.

## Negative points (lender credits)

You can also go the opposite direction — accept a **higher** rate in exchange for lender credits toward closing costs. This is called negative points or lender credits.

**Example:** Lender offers 7.5% with $4,000 in lender credits. You cover your closing costs without cash — but pay $57/month more forever. Break-even works in reverse: if you sell or refinance within 70 months, lender credits saved you money.

Lender credits make sense when:
- You're short on closing cost cash
- You expect to move or refinance within 3–5 years
- You're getting an adjustable-rate mortgage
