"Renting is throwing money away." You've heard it a hundred times. It's also not entirely true. Here's the honest math on buying vs renting in 2025 — with real numbers, not real estate agent talking points.

## The true cost of homeownership

Most people compare rent to just the mortgage P&I payment. That's the wrong comparison. The true monthly cost of owning includes items renters never pay:

| Cost | Monthly (on $450K home, 10% down) |
|------|----------------------------------|
| Mortgage P&I (7%, 30yr) | $2,694 |
| Property tax (1.1%) | $412 |
| Homeowner's insurance | $125 |
| PMI (0.7% on 90% LTV) | $236 |
| Maintenance (1% annually) | $375 |
| **True monthly cost** | **$3,842** |

If you're renting a comparable home for $2,500/month, buying costs you $1,342 more every month. That's $16,104/year more — not including the $45,000 down payment you tied up.

## What renters gain: opportunity cost

When buyers put $45,000 into a down payment, renters can invest that money. Historically, the S&P 500 returns ~10% annually. $45,000 growing at 10% for 10 years = $116,765. That's $71,765 in investment gains the buyer missed.

This is the **opportunity cost of homeownership** — rarely mentioned in "rent vs buy" debates.

## What buyers gain: equity and appreciation

Buyers build equity two ways: paying down principal and home price appreciation.

**On a $450,000 home with 10% down:**

| After | Remaining Loan | Home Value (3% appreciation) | Equity | Net Worth Gain |
|-------|---------------|------------------------------|--------|---------------|
| 3 years | $396,000 | $491,845 | $95,845 | +$50,845 |
| 5 years | $382,000 | $521,673 | $139,673 | +$94,673 |
| 10 years | $356,000 | $604,760 | $248,760 | +$203,760 |

The leverage effect is powerful: you control a $450,000 asset with $45,000 down. A 3% annual gain on the full value ($13,500/year) is a 30% return on your initial investment.

## The 5% rule — a quick filter

Financial planner Ben Felix popularized the **5% rule**: multiply the home's purchase price by 5% and divide by 12. If comparable rent is below this number, renting is likely the better financial choice.

**For a $500,000 home:** $500,000 × 5% ÷ 12 = **$2,083/month**

If you can rent a comparable home for less than $2,083, renting may win financially. The 5% accounts for property tax (1%), maintenance (1%), and cost of capital (3%).

## When buying clearly wins

- **You stay 5+ years:** The break-even point on most markets where buying beats renting
- **High-rent markets:** NYC, SF, Boston — where renting is extremely expensive
- **Appreciation markets:** Cities with strong job growth and limited housing supply
- **Rate drops after purchase:** Refinancing later can dramatically improve buying economics
- **You value stability:** Fixed payments, no landlord, no forced moves

## When renting clearly wins

- **You move within 3 years:** Transaction costs (closing costs + agent fees = 7–10%) kill short-term buying economics
- **High price-to-rent ratio:** Some markets have $800K homes renting for $2,500 — the math rarely works for buyers
- **Down payment better invested:** If you're in the accumulation phase and equity markets outperform housing
- **Life uncertainty:** Career change, relationship uncertainty, or desire for flexibility
- **Better local alternatives:** Some cities genuinely have affordable, quality rental stock

## The honest conclusion

Neither buying nor renting is universally better. The right answer depends on:

1. **How long you'll stay** (the most important factor)
2. **Your local price-to-rent ratio**
3. **Expected home appreciation** in your specific market
4. **What you'd do with the down payment** if renting
5. **Non-financial factors** — stability, customization, community roots
