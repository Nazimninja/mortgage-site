The mortgage interest deduction is one of the most-discussed tax benefits of homeownership — and one of the most misunderstood. Here is exactly how it works in 2025, who benefits, and how to calculate your actual tax savings.

## What is the mortgage interest deduction?

The IRS allows you to deduct the interest you pay on your mortgage from your taxable income — but only if you **itemize deductions** rather than taking the standard deduction. The deduction applies to interest on up to $750,000 of mortgage debt (for loans originated after Dec 15, 2017).

## The critical issue: standard deduction vs itemizing

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act nearly doubled the standard deduction, making it much harder for itemizing to beat the standard deduction for most homeowners.

**2025 standard deductions:**
- Single: $15,000
- Married filing jointly: $30,000
- Head of household: $22,500

For the mortgage interest deduction to benefit you, your **total itemized deductions** (mortgage interest + state/local taxes + charitable donations + other) must **exceed** your standard deduction.

## Who actually benefits?

**Likely benefits:** Homeowners with large mortgages, high incomes, significant state income taxes (SALT), and large charitable donations.

**Often doesn't benefit:** Homeowners with smaller mortgages, those in low-tax states, or those with modest other deductions.

**Example: $400,000 mortgage at 7%, single filer in Texas**

| Deduction | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Mortgage interest (Year 1) | $27,895 |
| Property tax (1.1% on $450K) | $4,950 |
| State income tax | $0 (Texas) |
| Charitable giving | $2,000 |
| **Total itemized** | **$34,845** |
| Standard deduction | $15,000 |
| **Benefit from itemizing** | **$19,845** |
| Tax savings (22% bracket) | **$4,366/year** |

**Same scenario, married couple:**

| Deduction | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Total itemized | $34,845 |
| Standard deduction (MFJ) | $30,000 |
| **Benefit from itemizing** | **$4,845** |
| Tax savings (22% bracket) | **$1,066/year** |

The married couple barely benefits. The single filer saves $4,366/year — meaningful but not life-changing.

## The $750,000 cap explained

You can only deduct interest on the first $750,000 of mortgage debt (down from $1,000,000 pre-2018 for existing loans). If your mortgage is $1,000,000, you can only deduct 75% of your interest.

**Grandfathered loans:** Mortgages originated before December 15, 2017 can still deduct interest on up to $1,000,000 of debt.

## How to calculate your actual tax savings

1. Find your total mortgage interest paid (reported on Form 1098 from your lender)
2. Add other itemizable deductions: property taxes (SALT cap: $10,000), charitable donations, etc.
3. Compare total to your standard deduction
4. If itemized > standard: the **difference** is your tax benefit
5. Multiply the difference by your marginal tax rate = actual tax savings

<div class="tip"><strong>Example:</strong> If itemizing saves you $8,000 over the standard deduction and you're in the 22% bracket: $8,000 × 22% = $1,760 actual tax savings. Not $8,000 — just $1,760.</div>

## Home equity loan interest

Interest on home equity loans and HELOCs is **only deductible if the funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve your home**. Using a HELOC for debt consolidation or a vacation does not qualify.

## The alternative minimum tax (AMT)

High-income homeowners subject to the AMT may not benefit from the mortgage interest deduction even if they itemize. Consult a CPA if your income exceeds $250,000 (single) or $300,000 (married).
