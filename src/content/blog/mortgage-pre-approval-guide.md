Pre-approval is the most important step before house hunting. Without it, you're wasting time touring homes you can't buy. With a strong pre-approval, you can make competitive offers immediately.

## Pre-qualification vs pre-approval: the critical difference

**Pre-qualification:** A quick estimate based on information you provide verbally or online. No credit check. No document review. Means almost nothing to sellers.

**Pre-approval:** A full credit check plus review of your income documents, assets, and employment. The lender issues a letter stating they will lend you up to a specific amount under specific conditions. This is what sellers and agents take seriously.

Always get pre-approved — never just pre-qualified.

## Documents you'll need

Gather these before starting the application:

**Income:**
- Last 2 years of federal tax returns (all pages, all schedules)
- Last 2 years of W-2s and/or 1099s
- Last 30 days of pay stubs (2 most recent)
- If self-employed: 2 years of business tax returns + year-to-date P&L

**Assets:**
- Last 2–3 months of bank statements (all accounts, all pages)
- Investment account statements
- Retirement account statements
- Source documentation for any large deposits (gift letters, asset sale receipts)

**Identification:**
- Government-issued photo ID (driver's license or passport)
- Social Security number

**Debts:**
- Current mortgage or rental information
- All monthly debt obligations (car loans, student loans, credit cards)

## What lenders actually look at

**Credit score:** Minimum 620 for conventional, 580 for FHA. Best rates require 740+. Lenders pull all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) and use the middle score.

**Debt-to-income ratio (DTI):** Your proposed housing payment + all monthly debts ÷ gross monthly income. Conventional max: 45–50%. FHA max: 57% in some cases.

**Employment history:** 2 years in the same field preferred. Recent job changes are okay if staying in the same industry. New job can be counted if it's in same field with pay stubs to prove it started.

**Down payment and reserves:** Where the money came from matters. Large recent deposits require paper trails. After closing, lenders want to see 2+ months of reserves.

## How to improve your pre-approval amount

**Before applying:**
- Pay down revolving credit card balances below 30% utilization
- Don't open any new credit accounts (hard inquiries lower your score temporarily)
- Don't close old credit cards (reduces available credit)
- Don't make any large purchases (car, furniture, etc.)
- Resolve any collections or disputes on your credit report

**At application:**
- Include all income sources (alimony, rental income, side income with 2-year history)
- Add a co-borrower if their income and credit strengthens the application
- Document all assets thoroughly

## How many lenders should you apply with?

Apply with **at least 3 lenders** within a 45-day window. Multiple mortgage inquiries within this window count as a single inquiry on your credit report — so shopping costs you nothing in credit score terms.

Compare:
- Interest rate quoted
- APR (includes fees — more accurate total cost comparison)
- Origination fees and closing cost estimates
- Loan officer responsiveness

The cheapest rate isn't always the best — lender reliability and communication matter enormously during the process.

## Common mistakes that delay or kill pre-approval

1. **Applying before gathering all documents** — incomplete applications slow everything
2. **Large undocumented deposits** — any deposit over 50% of monthly income requires paper trail
3. **Changing jobs during the process** — call your loan officer before making any career moves
4. **Making large purchases on credit** — buying furniture, appliances, or a car before closing
5. **Co-signing for someone else's loan** — increases your DTI immediately
6. **Moving money between accounts** — creates confusing paper trails; inform your LO before doing this

## What happens after pre-approval

Your pre-approval letter specifies the maximum loan amount and is typically valid for **60–90 days**. After that, you'll need to update financial documents and potentially re-pull credit.

Once you make an accepted offer, your loan enters underwriting — a deeper review of everything submitted during pre-approval. Stay in contact with your loan officer and respond to document requests within 24 hours.
