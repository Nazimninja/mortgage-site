The 15-year vs 30-year mortgage debate has a clear financial winner — but personal finance is personal. Here are the real numbers and when each term makes sense.

## The core tradeoff

A 15-year mortgage has a lower interest rate and shorter term, dramatically reducing total interest paid. The tradeoff: significantly higher monthly payment.

**On a $400,000 loan (2025 rate estimates):**

| Metric | 30-Year at 7.0% | 15-Year at 6.375% |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| Monthly P&I | $2,661 | $3,463 |
| Extra monthly cost | — | +$802 |
| Total interest paid | $558,036 | $223,340 |
| Interest saved | — | $334,696 |
| Paid off | 2055 | 2040 |

The 15-year saves $334,696 in interest — but costs $802 more every month. That's the whole decision.

## When the 15-year wins clearly

**You can comfortably afford the higher payment.** Comfortably means the payment leaves adequate emergency fund, retirement contributions, and quality of life — not just technically fits in your budget.

**You're disciplined with money.** The 30-year with extra payments strategy only works if you actually make the extra payments consistently. The 15-year forces the discipline automatically.

**You're in peak earning years.** Ages 40–55 often have peak income and lower expenses (kids grown, car paid off). This is the sweet spot for the 15-year.

**You want a guaranteed 6.375% return.** Paying down mortgage debt is a risk-free guaranteed return equal to your interest rate. In an uncertain market, that's attractive.

## When the 30-year wins

**You're early in your career.** If you're 28 with variable income, student loans, and career uncertainty, the lower 30-year payment gives financial flexibility you'll value enormously.

**You have high-interest debt.** Credit cards at 20%+ should always be paid before taking on a higher mortgage payment. Take the 30-year, pay off the cards, then make extra payments.

**You haven't maxed retirement accounts.** If you're not contributing enough to get your full 401k match or max your Roth IRA, the 30-year payment frees cash for these higher-priority uses.

**You're in a high-return investment environment.** Mathematically, if you can earn 9–10% in index funds vs 6.375% mortgage debt, investing the $802/month difference beats the 15-year.

## The 30-year with extra payments: best of both?

Many financial advisors recommend a 30-year with voluntary extra payments:
- Lock in the lower required payment (financial safety net)
- Pay extra when cash flow allows
- Skip extra payments during lean months without penalty
- Matches the flexibility of investing while building equity

**The real risk:** Most people don't actually make consistent extra payments. The 15-year removes the optionality — and for many people, that's exactly why it works.

## The math on investing vs paying off mortgage

Assume you take a 30-year and invest the $802/month difference instead:

| Scenario | After 15 Years | After 30 Years |
|----------|---------------|---------------|
| 15-year mortgage (mortgage free at 15) | $0 payment, own home free | Own home free, all cash flow available |
| 30-year + invest $802/month at 8% | $278,000 invested, still have mortgage | $1.1M invested, own home free |

At 30 years, the investor has $1.1M in investments AND a paid-off home. The 15-year owner has a paid-off home and whatever they invested in years 16–30.

**The investor wins** — if they actually invest the difference. Most people don't.

## Decision framework

Choose **15-year** if:
- Payment is under 25% of take-home pay
- You have 6 months emergency fund
- You're maxing retirement accounts
- You're motivated by guaranteed debt payoff

Choose **30-year** if:
- Payment flexibility matters (variable income, single earner)
- You have high-interest debt to eliminate first
- You're not yet maxing retirement accounts
- You're disciplined investors who will actually invest the difference
