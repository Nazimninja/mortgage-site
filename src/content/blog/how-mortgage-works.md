A mortgage is the largest financial commitment most Americans ever make. Yet most first-time buyers don't fully understand how one works until they're already in the middle of closing. Here is everything you need to know — before you sign anything.

## What is a mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan secured by real estate. You borrow money from a lender to buy a home, and the home itself serves as collateral. If you stop making payments, the lender can foreclose — take possession of the property and sell it to recover what you owe.

Most US mortgages are either 15 or 30 years. You make equal monthly payments throughout the entire term, though how much of each payment goes toward principal vs interest changes significantly over time.

## How mortgage interest works

Mortgage interest is calculated monthly on your remaining loan balance. In the early years, most of your payment goes toward interest. Over time, as your balance decreases, more goes toward principal. This is called **amortization**.

**Real example: $400,000 loan at 7%, 30-year fixed**

| Year | Monthly Payment | Interest Portion | Principal Portion | Remaining Balance |
|------|----------------|-----------------|------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | $2,661 | $2,333 | $328 | $396,063 |
| 5 | $2,661 | $2,195 | $466 | $376,547 |
| 10 | $2,661 | $1,997 | $664 | $343,020 |
| 15 | $2,661 | $1,742 | $919 | $298,868 |
| 20 | $2,661 | $1,408 | $1,253 | $240,498 |
| 25 | $2,661 | $969 | $1,692 | $162,048 |
| 30 | $2,661 | $15 | $2,646 | $0 |

Notice how in Year 1, only $328 of your $2,661 payment reduces your balance. By Year 25, over $1,692 goes to principal. This is why paying extra in the early years has such a dramatic impact.

## What is PITI?

PITI is your total monthly housing payment — the number lenders use to qualify you:

- **P**rincipal — reducing your loan balance
- **I**nterest — cost of borrowing
- **T**axes — property taxes collected monthly
- **I**nsurance — homeowner's insurance (plus PMI if applicable)

<div class="callout"><strong>Important:</strong> When comparing rent to a mortgage, always use your full PITI — not just the P&I payment. Taxes and insurance add 20–40% on top of your loan payment in most markets.</div>

## What is an escrow account?

Your lender almost always requires an **escrow account** — a separate account that holds your property tax and insurance payments. Each month, 1/12th of your annual tax and insurance bills is added to your mortgage payment. The lender pays these bills directly when due.

This protects the lender by ensuring taxes and insurance are always paid on time — preventing tax liens or an uninsured property. At closing, you'll typically prepay 2–3 months of taxes and insurance to fund the escrow account.

## What is amortization?

Amortization is the process of gradually paying off your loan through regular payments. Despite equal monthly payments, the split between principal and interest shifts significantly over time.

The formula lenders use:

```
Monthly Payment = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1]

Where: P = loan amount, r = monthly interest rate, n = total payments
```

For a $400,000 loan at 7% for 30 years:
- Monthly rate = 7% ÷ 12 = 0.5833%
- Total payments = 360
- Monthly P&I = $2,661

## Fixed rate vs adjustable rate (ARM)

**Fixed Rate:** Your interest rate never changes. Payment is 100% predictable. Best for buyers who plan to stay long-term or when rates are historically low relative to history.

**Adjustable Rate Mortgage (ARM):** Rate is fixed for an initial period (5/1, 7/1, or 10/1 ARM), then adjusts annually based on a market index (typically SOFR). Lower initial rate but payment uncertainty after the fixed period. Best if you plan to sell or refinance before adjustment begins.

<div class="warning"><strong>ARM risk:</strong> If rates rise significantly after your fixed period ends, your payment could increase by hundreds per month. Always model worst-case scenarios before choosing an ARM.</div>

## What happens at closing?

Closing is the final step where legal ownership transfers to you. Expect to spend 1–2 hours signing documents and paying closing costs.

**Typical closing costs (2–5% of loan amount):**

- Loan origination fee (0.5–1%)
- Appraisal ($400–700)
- Title search and insurance ($1,000–2,500)
- Government recording fees ($50–500)
- Attorney fees (required in some states)
- Prepaid items: first year insurance premium + 2–3 months property tax escrow

**On closing day, bring:**
- Government-issued photo ID
- Cashier's check or wire transfer for closing costs
- Proof of homeowner's insurance

## How much down payment do you need?

The minimum varies by loan type, but more down payment = lower monthly payment, lower interest rate, and potentially avoiding PMI.

**The 20% threshold matters:** Put down less than 20% on a conventional loan and you'll pay PMI — typically $50–$250/month depending on loan size and down payment. This disappears once you reach 20% equity.

<div class="tip"><strong>First-time buyer programs:</strong> Many states offer down payment assistance grants of $5,000–$25,000 for first-time buyers. Check your state housing finance agency before assuming you need the full down payment from savings alone.</div>

## How your credit score affects your mortgage rate

Your credit score is the single biggest factor determining your interest rate — more than your income, job stability, or assets.

| Credit Score | 30-Year Rate (Est.) | Monthly Payment ($400K) | Extra Cost vs 760+ |
|-------------|--------------------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| 760+ | 6.75% | $2,594 | — |
| 740–759 | 6.875% | $2,628 | +$34/mo |
| 720–739 | 7.125% | $2,697 | +$103/mo |
| 700–719 | 7.5% | $2,797 | +$203/mo |
| 680–699 | 7.875% | $2,899 | +$305/mo |
| 660–679 | 8.375% | $3,047 | +$453/mo |

A 100-point difference in credit score can cost you over $100,000 in extra interest over 30 years. If your score is below 740, consider taking 6–12 months to improve it before applying.
