Adjustable rate mortgages offer lower initial rates than fixed loans — sometimes 0.5–1.5% lower. That can mean hundreds per month in savings. But the rate adjusts after the fixed period, and your payment can rise significantly. Here is everything you need to know before choosing an ARM.

## How ARMs work

An ARM has two phases:

**Fixed period:** Rate stays the same (5, 7, or 10 years depending on the product).

**Adjustment period:** After the fixed period, rate adjusts annually based on a market index (usually SOFR — Secured Overnight Financing Rate) plus a margin set by your lender.

**ARM naming convention:** A 5/1 ARM has a 5-year fixed period, then adjusts every 1 year. A 7/6 ARM has a 7-year fixed period, then adjusts every 6 months.

## Rate caps: your protection against runaway payments

Every ARM has caps that limit how much the rate can change:

- **Initial adjustment cap:** How much the rate can change at the first adjustment. Typically 2–5%.
- **Periodic cap:** How much the rate can change at each subsequent adjustment. Typically 2%.
- **Lifetime cap:** Maximum total change over the life of the loan. Typically 5–6%.

**Example: 5/1 ARM at 6.0% with 2/2/5 caps**

| Scenario | Year 1–5 | Year 6 (first adjust) | Year 7 | Lifetime max |
|----------|---------|----------------------|--------|-------------|
| Rates stay same | 6.0% | 6.0% | 6.0% | 6.0% |
| Rates rise 1%/yr | 6.0% | 8.0% | 9.0% | 11.0% |
| Rates fall | 6.0% | Could decrease | Could decrease | 6.0% floor (usually) |

In a worst-case scenario, a 6.0% ARM could become 11.0% — turning a manageable payment into an unaffordable one.

## Real payment impact of rate changes

On a $400,000 5/1 ARM at 6.0%:

| Rate at Adjustment | Monthly P&I | Change from Initial |
|-------------------|------------|-------------------|
| 6.0% (no change) | $2,398 | +$0 |
| 7.0% (+1%) | $2,532 | +$134/mo |
| 8.0% (+2% — first cap) | $2,669 | +$271/mo |
| 10.0% (subsequent cap) | $2,953 | +$555/mo |
| 11.0% (lifetime max) | $3,100 | +$702/mo |

A worst-case adjustment to 11% adds $702/month. Can you absorb that without financial distress?

## When ARMs make genuine financial sense

**You're certain you'll sell or refinance before adjustment.** If you're buying a 5-year starter home and will definitely sell before year 5, a 5/1 ARM gives you 5 years of lower rate with no adjustment risk. This is the cleanest ARM use case.

**You're buying in a declining rate environment.** If rates are high and expected to fall, an ARM lets you benefit from future rate drops without refinancing.

**The rate difference is significant.** If an ARM is 1%+ lower than fixed and you're confident in your timeline, the savings are meaningful. If it's only 0.25% lower, the fixed is almost always better.

**You have significant income growth expected.** If your income will likely be 30–50% higher in 5 years, the payment risk from adjustment is manageable.

## When ARMs are dangerous

**You're buying your long-term home.** If you plan to stay 10+ years, the fixed rate wins almost always — even if ARM's initial rate looks attractive.

**You're at the edge of affordability.** If the payment you can afford is the ARM payment, not the fixed payment, the ARM is masking an affordability problem. Any rate increase could push you into distress.

**Rate environment is low/rising.** In a rising rate environment, your ARM adjustment will almost certainly go up — not down.

**You don't understand the caps.** Never take an ARM without fully understanding your worst-case payment scenario.

## ARM vs Fixed: the break-even analysis

Assume 5/1 ARM at 6.0% vs 30-year fixed at 7.0% on $400,000:

- **ARM monthly savings:** $263/month ($2,398 vs $2,661)
- **ARM savings over 5 years:** $15,780
- **If you sell at year 5:** ARM wins by $15,780
- **If rates spike at year 6:** ARM could cost more than fixed saved

The ARM is purely a bet that you'll sell/refinance before adjustment — or that rates will be favorable at adjustment time.
