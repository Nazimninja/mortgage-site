Both products let you borrow against your home's equity — but they work very differently. Choosing the wrong one can cost thousands.

## What is home equity?

Home equity = current home value minus remaining mortgage balance.

If your home is worth $500,000 and you owe $300,000, you have $200,000 in equity. Lenders typically let you borrow up to 80–85% of your home's value minus your mortgage — so in this case, up to $125,000–$150,000.

## Home equity loan: the basics

A home equity loan is a **lump-sum second mortgage** with a fixed interest rate and fixed monthly payment. You receive all the money at once and repay it over 5–30 years.

**Best for:** One-time, defined expenses where you know the exact amount — major home renovation, medical bills, or debt consolidation.

**2025 rates:** Approximately 8.5–10% for well-qualified borrowers (vs 7% for first mortgages).

**Pros:**
- Fixed rate = predictable payment
- Lump sum ideal for defined projects
- Potentially tax-deductible if used for home improvements

**Cons:**
- Higher rate than first mortgage
- Pay interest on full amount from day one
- Two mortgage payments (first + home equity loan)

## HELOC: the basics

A Home Equity Line of Credit is a **revolving credit line** secured by your home — similar to a credit card but with much lower rates. During the draw period (typically 5–10 years), you borrow as needed and pay interest only on what you've used.

**Best for:** Ongoing or uncertain expenses — phased home improvements, emergency fund backup, tuition paid semester by semester.

**2025 rates:** Most HELOCs are variable rate, currently ~8–9% for well-qualified borrowers.

**Pros:**
- Only pay interest on what you borrow
- Flexibility to draw and repay repeatedly
- Often no closing costs (or low)
- Good emergency backup (better to have and not need)

**Cons:**
- Variable rate = payment uncertainty
- Discipline required — easy to overborrow
- Lender can reduce/freeze line during market downturns
- Repayment period after draw period can be jarring (payment jumps when you must pay principal)

## Side-by-side comparison

| Feature | Home Equity Loan | HELOC |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| Structure | Lump sum | Revolving credit line |
| Rate type | Fixed | Variable (usually) |
| Payments during draw | Principal + Interest | Interest only |
| Best for | Known one-time expense | Ongoing/uncertain expenses |
| Current 2025 rate | ~8.5–10% | ~8–9% |
| Closing costs | $500–$3,000 | $0–$1,000 |
| Tax deductibility | If for home improvements | If for home improvements |

## Tax deductibility rules (2025)

Interest on home equity loans and HELOCs is only deductible if the funds are used to **buy, build, or substantially improve your home**. The IRS eliminated the deduction for personal use (debt consolidation, vacations, tuition) in 2018.

Even for home improvement use, you must itemize deductions — and with the high 2025 standard deduction, most borrowers won't benefit.

## When NOT to use home equity

- **Discretionary spending:** Vacations, cars, lifestyle upgrades. You're converting unsecured debt into secured debt backed by your home.
- **Declining market:** If home values are falling, borrowing against equity risks being underwater (owing more than home is worth).
- **Unstable income:** Both products require monthly payments. Job loss with a HELOC or home equity loan can accelerate financial distress.

## Better alternatives to consider first

Before borrowing against home equity:
- **Personal loan:** For amounts under $50,000, personal loan rates (8–15%) are often competitive, with no risk to your home
- **0% intro APR credit cards:** For amounts under $20,000, a 0% introductory card (12–21 months) costs nothing if paid off in time
- **Cash-out refinance:** If you need to refinance anyway and rates are favorable, rolling everything into one mortgage can be cleaner
