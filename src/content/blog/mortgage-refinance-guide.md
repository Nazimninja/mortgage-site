Refinancing replaces your current mortgage with a new one. Done right, it saves tens of thousands. Done wrong, it costs thousands in fees with no meaningful benefit. Here's how to tell the difference.

## What is refinancing?

Refinancing means paying off your existing mortgage by taking out a new one — typically at a lower interest rate, different term, or to access your home equity (cash-out). You go through the full mortgage application process again: credit check, income verification, appraisal, and closing.

Closing costs: typically $3,000–$10,000 (2–5% of loan amount). This upfront cost is what makes break-even analysis critical.

## The 1% rule — and why it's outdated

You may have heard "refinance when you can lower your rate by 1%." This was useful shorthand in the era of smaller, shorter loans. Today it's too simplistic.

What actually matters is your **monthly savings vs closing costs** — and how long you'll stay in the home.

## Break-even analysis: the only calculation that matters

**Break-even months = Closing costs ÷ Monthly savings**

If you save $200/month and closing costs are $6,000: break-even is 30 months (2.5 years).

If you plan to stay in the home longer than 30 months → refinancing makes sense.
If you might move within 30 months → the math doesn't work.

| Closing Costs | Monthly Savings | Break-Even | Verdict |
|--------------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| $6,000 | $100 | 60 months | Too long for most |
| $6,000 | $200 | 30 months | Good if staying 3+ years |
| $6,000 | $300 | 20 months | Excellent deal |
| $6,000 | $500 | 12 months | Refinance immediately |
| $4,000 | $200 | 20 months | Strong case |

## Types of refinancing

**Rate-and-term refinance:** Change your interest rate, loan term, or both. Most common refinance type. Goal: lower monthly payment or pay off faster.

**Cash-out refinance:** Borrow more than your current balance and take the difference in cash. Your loan increases, but you receive funds for home improvements, debt consolidation, or investments. Rate is typically 0.125–0.25% higher than rate-and-term.

**Cash-in refinance:** Bring cash to closing to reduce your loan balance. Useful to remove PMI, qualify for a better rate, or reduce payment.

**Streamline refinance:** Available on FHA and VA loans with reduced documentation requirements. No appraisal needed in most cases.

## When refinancing makes clear sense

- **Rate drop of 0.75%+:** With a $350,000 loan, a 0.75% rate drop saves ~$157/month. Break-even on $6,000 closing costs: 38 months.
- **Credit score improved significantly:** If your score rose 80+ points since you got your mortgage, you may qualify for a materially better rate.
- **Removing PMI:** Refinancing when you've hit 20% equity eliminates PMI — often $150–$300/month savings on top of any rate benefit.
- **Switching from ARM to fixed:** If you have an adjustable-rate mortgage and rates are rising, locking in a fixed rate provides payment certainty.
- **Shortening your term:** Refinancing from 30-year to 15-year dramatically reduces total interest if you can afford the higher payment.

## When refinancing is a bad idea

- **Moving soon:** If you're selling within 2–3 years, you likely won't break even on closing costs.
- **Late in your loan:** In years 20–30, most of your payment is principal, not interest. Resetting to a new 30-year loan restarts the amortization clock.
- **Extending your term for payment relief:** Refinancing a 25-year remaining balance to a new 30-year reduces your payment but significantly increases total interest paid.
- **Prepayment penalties:** Rare on modern mortgages, but check your loan documents before refinancing.
- **Rolling closing costs into loan when near breakeven:** Adding $6,000 to your balance partially offsets your interest savings.

## Cash-out refinancing: is it worth it?

Cash-out refinancing makes financial sense when:
- The purpose creates value (home improvements that increase equity)
- You're consolidating high-interest debt (credit cards at 20%+ → mortgage at 7%)
- The rate difference is modest

Be cautious when:
- Using cash-out for consumption (vacations, cars, discretionary spending)
- You're extending your loan term significantly
- The new rate is more than 0.5% higher than your current rate

<div class="warning"><strong>Risk:</strong> Cash-out refinancing converts unsecured debt (credit cards) into secured debt (mortgage). If you can't pay a credit card, your credit suffers. If you can't pay your mortgage, you lose your home.</div>

## How to get the best refinance rate

1. **Shop at least 3 lenders** — rates vary by 0.25–0.5% between lenders for the same borrower
2. **Check credit unions and online lenders** — often lower rates than big banks
3. **Improve your credit score first** — even 3–6 months of credit repair can save thousands
4. **Consider buying points** — paying 1% of loan amount upfront to lower rate by ~0.25%. Worth it if staying long-term.
5. **Lock your rate** — get a rate lock for 30–60 days once you find your lender
