Your credit score is the single biggest lever for getting a lower mortgage rate. Going from 680 to 760 can save $50,000+ over the life of a loan. Here is exactly how to raise your score as fast as possible.

## Why credit score matters so much for mortgages

| Credit Score | Estimated 30-yr Rate | Monthly Payment ($400K) | Total Extra Cost vs 760+ |
|-------------|---------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| 760+ | 6.75% | $2,594 | — |
| 740–759 | 6.875% | $2,628 | +$12,240 |
| 720–739 | 7.125% | $2,697 | +$36,720 |
| 700–719 | 7.5% | $2,797 | +$72,360 |
| 680–699 | 7.875% | $2,899 | +$109,800 |
| 660–679 | 8.375% | $3,047 | +$163,080 |

A 100-point improvement in credit score can save over $100,000 over 30 years. It is almost always worth delaying your home purchase by 6–12 months to improve your score.

## How mortgage lenders check your credit

Lenders pull all three credit bureaus — **Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion** — and use your **middle score** (not the average, not the highest). If your scores are 690, 710, 730, they use 710.

This means improving your score at one bureau may not help if a different bureau is the middle score. Focus on all three.

## The 5 factors that determine your score

| Factor | Weight | Action Priority |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Payment history | 35% | Highest — never miss payments |
| Credit utilization | 30% | High — pay down balances |
| Length of credit history | 15% | Medium — don't close old cards |
| Credit mix | 10% | Low — don't open new accounts for this |
| New inquiries | 10% | Low — avoid unnecessary applications |

## Fast wins: what moves the needle quickly

### 1. Pay down credit card balances (biggest impact, fastest)

Credit utilization — the percentage of available credit you're using — accounts for 30% of your score. Keeping utilization below 10% gives the best scores; above 30% starts hurting significantly.

**Example:** You have $20,000 in available credit across all cards. Owing $8,000 = 40% utilization (hurts). Paying it to $2,000 = 10% utilization (excellent). This single action can raise your score 30–60 points in one billing cycle.

Pay balances down before your statement closing date — that's when balances are reported to credit bureaus.

### 2. Dispute errors on your credit report (free, potentially huge)

Studies show ~25% of credit reports contain errors significant enough to affect lending decisions. Pull your free report at **AnnualCreditReport.com** and check for:

- Accounts that aren't yours (possible identity theft or reporting error)
- Late payments you actually paid on time
- Accounts showing open that you closed
- Incorrect balances or credit limits
- Duplicate accounts

Dispute errors directly with each bureau online. Errors must be investigated within 30 days. Successful disputes can raise scores 20–50+ points.

### 3. Become an authorized user

Ask a family member with excellent credit and low utilization to add you as an authorized user on their credit card. Their positive history appears on your report — without you needing to use the card at all.

Effect: Can raise score 20–50 points depending on the account's history and your current profile. Works fastest for people with thin credit files.

### 4. Pay all bills on time — starting now

Payment history is 35% of your score. A single 30-day late payment can drop your score 60–110 points — and stays on your report for 7 years.

Set up autopay for at least minimum payments on every account. Never miss a payment again. A perfect payment history for 12–24 months significantly improves your score.

### 5. Don't close old credit cards

Length of credit history = 15% of your score. Closing an old card reduces your average account age and eliminates available credit (raising your utilization ratio). Keep old cards open even if you don't use them.

## What NOT to do before applying for a mortgage

**Don't open new credit accounts.** Each hard inquiry drops your score 5–10 points temporarily. New accounts lower your average age of accounts.

**Don't close credit cards.** Reduces available credit, raising your utilization ratio.

**Don't make large purchases on credit.** Increases utilization right when you need it lowest.

**Don't co-sign for anyone.** Their debt becomes your debt in lender's eyes — increases your DTI.

**Don't apply for financing anywhere.** Car dealerships, furniture stores, electronics — all trigger hard inquiries.

## Realistic timelines

| Action | Score Impact | Timeframe |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Pay down credit cards | +30–60 pts | 1–2 billing cycles (30–60 days) |
| Dispute and fix errors | +20–50 pts | 30–60 days |
| Become authorized user | +20–50 pts | 30–60 days |
| 12 months perfect payments | +20–40 pts | 12 months |
| Reduce inquiries (just wait) | +5–15 pts | 12 months |

For a major score improvement (100+ points), allow **12–18 months** of consistent effort. For moderate improvement (40–60 points), allow **3–6 months**.

## The mortgage credit pull strategy

When you're ready to apply, get all your mortgage pre-approvals within a **45-day window**. Multiple mortgage inquiries within this window count as a single inquiry under FICO scoring models. Shop freely — it costs you nothing.
