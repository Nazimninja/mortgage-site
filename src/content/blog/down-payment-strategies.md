The down payment is the biggest barrier for most first-time buyers. Here are the strategies that actually work — including programs most buyers don't know exist.

## How much down payment do you actually need?

Spoiler: not 20%. That's a guideline, not a requirement.

| Loan Type | Minimum Down | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Conventional | 3% | PMI required until 20% equity |
| FHA | 3.5% (580+ score) | MIP for life of loan if <10% down |
| VA | 0% | Veterans only, no PMI ever |
| USDA | 0% | Rural areas, income limits |
| Conventional (no PMI) | 20% | Best long-term economics |

On a $350,000 home, the difference between 3% ($10,500) and 20% ($70,000) is $59,500. That's years of saving for most families.

## Down payment assistance programs (DPA)

This is the most underutilized resource in home buying. Thousands of programs exist offering grants and forgivable loans.

**Federal programs:**
- **HUD-approved housing counseling** — free guidance on available programs
- **USDA Section 502 loans** — 0% down for qualifying rural buyers

**State programs:** Every state has a housing finance agency offering DPA. Examples:
- California: CalHFA — up to 3.5% grant
- Texas: TSAHC — up to 5% in down payment assistance
- New York: SONYMA — low rates + down payment assistance
- Florida: FL Housing — up to $10,000 forgivable loan

**Local programs:** Many cities offer $5,000–$50,000 in assistance for buying within city limits. Search "[your city] first time home buyer assistance."

<div class="tip"><strong>Key fact:</strong> DPA programs don't require you to be poor — many have income limits up to 120–140% of area median income. A household earning $100,000+ may still qualify in many markets.</div>

## Gift funds from family

Conventional and FHA loans allow down payment funds to come from gifts from family members (parents, siblings, grandparents). Requirements:
- Gift letter signed by donor stating funds are a gift (not a loan)
- Documentation of transfer from donor's account
- Some programs require 5% to come from your own funds for conventional loans with <20% down

## 401k and IRA withdrawal options

**First-time homebuyer IRA exception:** Withdraw up to $10,000 (lifetime) from a traditional IRA for a first home purchase without the 10% early withdrawal penalty. You still owe income tax on the withdrawal.

**Roth IRA:** Contributions (not earnings) can always be withdrawn tax and penalty-free. Earnings can be withdrawn penalty-free after 5 years for first-home purchase up to $10,000.

**401k loan:** Many 401k plans allow loans of up to 50% of vested balance (max $50,000). Repaid over 5 years with interest (paid to yourself). Risk: if you leave your job, the loan may become due immediately.

<div class="warning"><strong>Caution:</strong> Raiding retirement accounts for a home down payment sets back your retirement significantly. Model the long-term impact before doing this.</div>

## Accelerated savings strategies

**High-yield savings account (HYSA):** Park your down payment savings in an HYSA earning 4.5–5% APY vs 0.01% in a traditional savings account. $30,000 earns $1,500/year in an HYSA vs $3 in a regular account.

**Automate savings:** Set an automatic transfer to your HYSA every payday. Treat it like a bill. Even $500/month = $6,000/year + interest.

**Cut one major expense:** Eliminating car payments, downsizing to a cheaper apartment for 1–2 years, or cutting dining/entertainment by $500/month can accelerate your timeline by 2–3 years.

**Side income earmarked for down payment:** Freelance, overtime, selling items — put 100% of extra income toward your down payment fund.

## The 3% vs 20% down decision

Putting down less than 20% means paying PMI — but it also means buying sooner and starting to build equity while paying PMI.

**Scenario: $400,000 home in a market appreciating 4% annually**

| Option | Time to Buy | PMI Paid | Equity at Year 5 |
|--------|------------|---------|-----------------|
| 3% down, buy now | 0 months | ~$14,000 | ~$127,000 |
| 20% down, save first | 3.5 years | $0 | ~$48,000 |

Despite paying $14,000 in PMI, buying sooner captured $127,000 in equity vs $48,000 — a massive difference in a rising market. In a flat or declining market, the math shifts toward saving the full 20%.
